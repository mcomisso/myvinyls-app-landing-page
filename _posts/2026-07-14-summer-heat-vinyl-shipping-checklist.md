There is a particular kind of summer record anxiety: the tracking page says "out for delivery", the temperature outside is climbing, and the parcel is probably sitting in a van, depot, porch, hallway, or shop counter somewhere between you and the turntable.

Most records survive summer shipping just fine. Vinyl is not made of glass. But heat, pressure, sunlight, poor packing, and long delivery windows are a bad combination, especially during a run of hot weather. The Met Office reported that the UK had already recorded an unusual sequence of 35°C days across May, June, and July 2026, with parts of England and Wales still expected to stay above 30°C into mid-July. That is exactly the sort of week when collectors should think twice before treating every order like business as usual.

This does not mean you need to stop buying records in summer. It means you need a better checklist.

## Decide Whether the Record Can Wait

The first question is not "will this record warp?" It is "does this order need to move this week?"

If it is a common repress, a standard black copy, or a title that several retailers still have in stock, waiting a few days is often the cleanest move. The record will still exist after the heat breaks. You avoid the most stressful delivery window, and you make it less likely that your first experience with the record is photographing a dish warp for a support email.

If it is genuinely limited, signed, already allocated, or a used copy you have been hunting for months, the decision changes. In that case, the aim is not to avoid all risk. The aim is to control the parts you can control: seller, packaging, service speed, delivery address, and inspection.

As a rule, pause low-urgency purchases during extreme heat and be more deliberate with high-urgency ones.

## Choose the Seller, Not Just the Price

During normal weather, the cheapest copy can be tempting. During hot weather, the seller matters more.

Look for shops and sellers that already understand records as fragile objects. Good signs include proper LP mailers, stiffeners, clear grading notes, quick dispatch, tracking, and sensible customer service language around damage. Bad signs include vague marketplace listings, slow dispatch windows, no tracking, and sellers who ship expensive records like ordinary books.

Disc Makers' shipping advice highlights the familiar damage triangle: heat, humidity, and uneven pressure. It also recommends rigid packaging, insulation where possible, avoiding hot environments before handoff, and faster shipping to reduce time in transit. That is common-sense advice, but it is easy to ignore when a cheaper listing is staring at you.

For valuable records, paying a little more to buy from a specialist shop can be the cheaper decision in the end.

## Upgrade Speed When the Record Matters

Slow shipping is not automatically dangerous, but it increases the number of places where a parcel can sit in poor conditions. Depots, vehicles, lockups, porches, and collection points are not climate-controlled listening rooms.

If the record is valuable, rare, or difficult to replace, consider paying for a faster tracked service. Easyship's shipping guide makes the same practical point: heat and humidity can damage records in transit, and faster delivery reduces exposure time. That does not guarantee a perfect copy, but it reduces the amount of time the package spends in the danger zone.

This is especially worth considering for international orders. A domestic parcel may only have one or two hot handoff points. An international parcel can pass through more facilities, longer customs delays, and less predictable weekend holds.

If you would be upset enough to chase a replacement, the extra shipping cost may be worth it.

## Send It Somewhere You Can Receive It Quickly

The worst summer delivery address is the one where a parcel sits outside for six hours.

If you work from home, use home. If you are out all day, consider a workplace, a trusted neighbour, a parcel shop with indoor storage, or a delivery locker only if it is shaded and not baking in direct sun. The goal is simple: shorten the time between "delivered" and "indoors".

Avoid car boots. Avoid conservatories. Avoid windowsills. Avoid leaving the parcel in direct sun "just for a bit". Direct sunlight can heat a dark mailer or record sleeve far beyond the air temperature, and pressure inside the package can turn softening into a visible warp.

Once the parcel arrives, bring it inside and let it settle at room temperature before playing. The Library of Congress advises allowing audiovisual materials from cool storage to acclimate before playback; the same patience is useful in reverse when a record has been through heat. Do not open a very warm parcel, pull the record out immediately, and put it straight on a turntable.

## Inspect Before You File It Away

Open summer deliveries the same day if you can. You do not need to be dramatic about it, but you do need to know what arrived.

Check the mailer first. Is it bent, crushed, wet, or softened? Photograph it before opening if anything looks wrong. Then check the sleeve corners, seams, and spine. If the seller shipped the disc outside the jacket, that may prevent seam splits; if not, pay closer attention to the top and side seams.

Then check the record on a flat, clean surface. Look for obvious dish warps, edge warps, and ripples. A slight storage warp is not always audible, but a new record that arrived badly warped should be reported quickly, especially if the seller or carrier has a damage window.

Do not try aggressive flattening tricks on a valuable record unless you know what you are doing. Heat caused the problem; more heat used casually can make it worse. If the record is new, start with the seller's return process. If it is used and irreplaceable, ask a specialist before attempting any treatment.

## Store the New Arrival Properly

The delivery risk does not end when the box is open. A record that survived the courier can still be damaged by a bad shelf.

Keep LPs vertical, supported, and away from direct sunlight. Do not leave new arrivals in a leaning "to file later" pile for a week. Do not stack heavy mailers flat on top of each other in a hot room. Do not keep records in a loft, garage, shed, or car unless you are comfortable treating them as replaceable.

For a working home collection, aim for a stable, comfortable room rather than chasing archive perfection. The Library of Congress gives conservative handling guidance for recorded sound materials, and archival sources generally emphasise stability, cleanliness, low light, and controlled temperature and humidity. For collectors, the practical version is: upright, shaded, dry, clean, and boring.

Boring storage is good storage.

## Track Summer Orders Like a Collector

Hot-weather buying is easier when you know what is on the way and why you bought it.

In My Vinyls, add a note to high-risk orders before they arrive: seller, format, pressing, price paid, shipping service, expected delivery date, and whether it is replaceable. If the record arrives with a warp, seam split, noisy side, or perfect condition, log that too. Over time, you will learn which shops pack well, which couriers are reliable in your area, and which kinds of purchases are worth delaying.

That history is more useful than a vague memory of "I think this seller was fine last time."

## The Simple Heatwave Rule

If a record is common, wait. If it is rare, upgrade the shipping and receive it quickly. If it arrives warm, let it settle. If it arrives damaged, document it before the trail goes cold.

Summer heat does not have to stop a collection. It just rewards the collectors who slow down for five minutes before clicking buy.
