Record Store Day 2026 is done. The queues cleared, the Instagram posts went up, and if you didn't get the release you wanted, the secondary market was already doing its thing by Sunday morning.

But here's something that gets lost in the RSD noise: not every release on the official list was a one-day-only event. Some were marked "RSD First" — meaning the title might get a wider release later. And if you missed it on April 18th, you might not need to pay Discogs prices to get it.

## What "RSD First" Actually Means

Record Store Day uses two labels on releases:

**RSD Exclusive** — this is a one-time pressing. If you don't get it on RSD day, you are relying on secondary market, a shop that held stock, or very good luck. These typically don't come back in the same form.

**RSD First** — the title is appearing on vinyl for the first time, but the label reserves the right to release it more widely later. The RSD drop is the initial window, not the only window.

The distinction matters. A release marked "RSD First" is worth watching if you missed it on the day, particularly if the asking price on Discogs is substantially above retail.

## The Releases Worth Watching

Based on the RSD 2026 list, several releases with the "RSD First" designation are worth tracking:

**Steely Dan — "Alive in America" (RSD First)** — their first official live album gets its vinyl debut for RSD 2026, originally released in 1995. If you're a Steely Dan fan who missed out, patience might pay off.

**Talking Heads — "The CBS/Columbia Demos" (RSD First)** — 7,000 copies on 2xLP, vinyl debut for these demos. Given the catalogue depth and the Talking Heads collector market, this one has a genuine case for wider release given the "First" designation.

**a-ha — "Analogue 20th Anniversary Deluxe Edition" (RSD First)** — an anniversary reissue with deluxe packaging. Anniversary editions routinely get wider releases once the initial window closes.

The key thing to check: the RSD Drops program. Some titles from the 2026 list that didn't dominate the headlines are being held back for separate release dates later in the year. This isn't always clearly communicated, so following the social media of the shops you trust — or checking the RSD official site — is the best way to know what's coming.

## The Timing Question

The honest answer is that "RSD First" doesn't come with a guaranteed wider release date. Labels decide later, sometimes months later. If a title sold strongly at RSD, a label might choose to do a repress for wider sale. If it sold modestly, they might not.

The pattern that tends to repeat: titles with high streaming numbers and active fanbases (Taylor Swift, K-pop releases) rarely get wider represses at the same price point. Catalogue releases with less obvious mainstream demand are more likely to return in some form.

## What to Do Right Now

If you're hunting for something that was RSD First:

Check the shops directly. Some independents put leftover RSD stock online the week after the event. It doesn't always get announced loudly — check the online stores of your local shops rather than waiting for an email.

Sign up for notifications from labels you care about. If a repress is planned, it's usually announced there first.

Wait before paying secondary market prices on "RSD First" titles unless you genuinely can't wait. The Discogs spike on the Monday after RSD is often temporary for titles with wider availability coming. If the label is planning a repress, the price will likely soften.
