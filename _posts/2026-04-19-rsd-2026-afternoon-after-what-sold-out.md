If you were in a queue outside a record shop at 7 AM yesterday, you already lived it. If you weren't — here's what you're walking into today.

Record Store Day 2026 is done. The dust is settling, Discogs is already moving, and if you're reading this trying to figure out what actually happened: welcome to the aftermath.

The UK had a particularly notable RSD this year. UK ambassador Olivia Dean — who won Grammy Best New Artist — had her BBC Radio 1 Live Lounge performance pressed to vinyl as the UK RSD release, a direct link between one of the UK's biggest current artists and the independent shop ecosystem. The UK vinyl market itself continues to surge: it grew 18.6% in value in 2025 with over 7.5 million discs sold and £238 million in revenue, according to the NME/DERA report. Independent UK shops were part of nearly 3,000 stores globally participating in RSD 2026 — a reminder that behind the global headlines, the local record shop is still the heart of the event.

## What Sold Fastest

Taylor Swift's "Elizabeth Taylor" 7-inch was the title everyone expected to dominate conversations, and by most reports it did. The violet glitter pressing was reported as likely to sell out at most independent shops within the first hour. What's interesting is that Swift's team declined to reveal the pressing quantity — a pattern that started with last year's "Fortnight" single, which was so heavily stocked at some stores it didn't sell out for days or even weeks. The speculation online is split: either the pressing was genuinely enormous, or Taylor Swift's fanbase would have bought double that amount anyway. Either way, if you wanted it and didn't get there early, you're probably paying more now.

The K-pop Demon Hunters soundtrack — tied to Netflix's hugely popular anime property — got two separate exclusive pressings for RSD 2026, each with different cover art. Both appear to have moved quickly in shops that stocked them. The Demon Hunters release is a good example of the newer RSD dynamic: a property with enormous streaming numbers translating into real physical demand, but at a scale that still leaves independent shops as the only access point.

Neil Young with Chrome Hearts, "As Time Explodes" (6,000 clear vinyl copies, RSD First) was another early mover. Given the scale of the 2025 Chrome Hearts tour — which was extensive across Europe and North America and drew some of the strongest reviews Young has received in decades — a live album from those shows was always going to attract both serious Young collectors and the Chrome Hearts crossover audience.

Bruce Springsteen's "Live From Asbury Park 2024" (5,050 LP copies, black vinyl, RSD Exclusive) was the kind of release that RSD does better than anyone: a first physical release of a live show that was previously only on Nugs, spread across five LPs, in a proper gatefold package. Five thousand copies is genuinely small for a Springsteen release. That one went fast.

Jeff Buckley's "Live à L'Olympia" arriving on vinyl for the first time had timing working in its favour — "Lover, You Should've Come Over" had just charted on the Hot 100 more than three decades after release, which brought a wave of new attention to an artist who died before that kind of moment became possible. The vinyl release of this live recording was always going to punch above its usual collector weight.

## What Didn't Sell Out (and That's Fine)

The interesting thing about RSD is that the obvious headline titles aren't always the ones that actually vanish first. Some of the smaller pressings — particularly jazz reissues and obscure indie releases — sold out in shops that ordered them, but never generated the social media traffic that the Taylor Swift release did.

Talking Heads' "The CBS/Columbia Demos" (7,000 copies, 2xLP, vinyl debut) and The Who's "A Quick One" 60th Anniversary Edition (7,000 copies, remastered mono) were the kind of catalogue releases that serious collectors were actually chasing. These aren't the titles that make noise online, but they're the ones that vanish from the right shops without anyone posting about it.

## The Secondary Market, Right Now

By this morning, Discogs was already reflecting the RSD premium on a handful of titles. This is predictable and worth understanding rather than getting upset about.

The dynamic is consistent: titles with high demand and genuinely small pressings spike on Saturday morning, peak around Sunday-Monday when the "I missed it" impulse is highest, then settle over the following weeks as people who bought decide whether to keep or flip. A release that sold for £25-£30 in the shop is often £50-£80 on Discogs within 24 hours. By May, some of that premium has usually softened — not always, but often enough that patience is a legitimate strategy.

The releases that hold elevated secondary prices longest are the ones with genuine long-term collector communities: Taylor Swift variants, certain K-pop titles, and releases tied to artists with active, engaged fanbases who treat physical editions as cultural objects rather than just music purchases. A standard repress of something that was only "rare" because of low pressing quantities tends to correct faster.

If you missed something and the secondary price is bothering you: wait. Unless it's a genuinely tiny pressing with no reissue planned, the window for "I'll never find this" is usually shorter than the anxiety suggests.

## What to Check Today

A few things worth knowing if you're hunting today or this week:

Many shops put leftover RSD stock on their own websites in the days after the event. This isn't always announced loudly — check the social media of your local independent stores rather than waiting for an email. Some independent shops also hold back copies for in-store customers who asked to be notified.

The RSD Drops programme — titles released later in the year rather than on the April date — means some of the titles on the 2026 list that didn't get the attention of the headline acts will get their moment later. If you missed a smaller release and it had "RSD First" rather than "RSD Exclusive," there's a reasonable chance it gets a wider release.

Finally: shops that had a busy RSD often discount their regular inventory over the weekend as part of the event. If you came for the exclusives and left with nothing, it's worth browsing the non-RSD sections today or tomorrow. Some of the best finds on RSD weekend are never the point of the day.
