New Order reissues are awkward in a good way. This is a band where the singles matter, the 12-inch mixes matter, the artwork matters, and the exact version of a track can change the whole mood of a record shelf.

That is why this week's *The Best Of / The Rest Of* reissue is more interesting than a standard greatest-hits repress. Rough Trade lists the new editions for July 17, 2026, including a 2LP *The Best Of*, a 3LP *The Rest Of*, a 2CD version, and a 4CD deluxe set. The official New Order store also has black vinyl and exclusive colour versions, including a blue 2LP *Best Of* and a pink 3LP *Rest Of*.

The useful question is which version actually fits your collection.

## What Is Coming on July 17

The core vinyl split is simple. *The Best Of New Order* is the hits set: a 2LP compilation built around the band's singles from the mid-1980s onward, including *True Faith-94*, *Bizarre Love Triangle-94*, *Regret*, *The Perfect Kiss*, *Blue Monday-88*, and *World in Motion*.

The official store says the new 2LP blue edition is cut at Abbey Road Studios and marks the first time this compilation has been reissued and remastered on vinyl. Rough Trade lists the standard 2LP as double 140 gram vinyl.

*The Rest Of New Order* is the remix companion. Rough Trade describes the new version as a 3LP set, also with remastered audio cut at Abbey Road, bringing together all 12 songs from the CD and original vinyl release for the first time. That matters because the remix record is not just a footnote. It is part of how New Order's catalogue moved through clubs, DJs, and 1990s remix culture.

Classic Pop's coverage also confirms the July 17 Warners release date and notes the separate vinyl formats, Abbey Road cuts, and exclusive blue and pink colour options through the New Order store.

## Buy the 2LP Best Of If You Want the Everyday New Order Shelf

For most collectors, the 2LP *Best Of* is the cleanest purchase.

It gives you the familiar singles in one playable package without turning the decision into a format maze. If your collection already has *Power, Corruption & Lies*, *Low-Life*, *Brotherhood*, *Technique*, or *Republic*, this compilation still earns a place because New Order's single edits and remixes are part of the story. The album is not trying to replace the studio LPs. It is more like a map of the band's public-facing language: bright hooks, club architecture, melancholy melodies, and a rhythm section that keeps pulling everything toward the dancefloor.

This is also the best option if you want something you will actually play. A 2LP compilation is easy to reach for, easy to file, and less demanding than a deluxe CD box or a remix-focused 3LP set. If you are buying one version for listening, start here.

The choice between black and blue vinyl should come down to price, shipping, and how much the colour matters to you. The official blue version is attractive as a store exclusive, but colour alone should not make the decision. If a local or European retailer has the black 2LP at a better delivered price, with easier returns, that may be the smarter collector copy.

## Buy the 3LP Rest Of If You Actually Collect Remixes

The 3LP *Rest Of* is a different kind of purchase. It is less about "the songs everyone knows" and more about a very specific version of New Order: the band as source material for club mixes, extended forms, and 1990s dance culture.

Rough Trade's track listing includes mixes such as *World (Perfecto Mix)*, *Blue Monday (Hardfloor Mix)*, *True Faith (Shep Pettibone Remix)*, *Confusion (Pump Panel Reconstruction Mix)*, *Bizarre Love Triangle (Armand Van Helden Mix)*, and *Temptation (CJ Bolland Mix)*.

That is the test. If those names make the record feel more useful, the 3LP makes sense. If they look like homework, buy the *Best Of* first.

Remix albums can be brilliant, but they ask for a different listening habit. You may play one side for a DJ-adjacent mood, compare a mix against an original 12-inch, or file it near other remix culture records rather than next to the main albums. If your shelf is mostly studio albums, the 3LP may become a beautiful object you rarely pull out.

## Be Careful With the Colour Variant Reflex

The official store lists colour exclusives, including blue for *The Best Of* and pink for *The Rest Of*. They fit the campaign visually, and for some collectors that will be enough.

Just separate the reasons clearly:

- Buy the colour version if you like the look and the delivered price is reasonable.
- Buy the black version if you want the simplest listening copy from a retailer you trust.
- Buy both only if you deliberately collect New Order variants.
- Do not buy a colour copy just because the word "exclusive" is doing all the work.

With a catalogue reissue, the practical details matter more than the panic details. Who is shipping it? How are returns handled if the record is warped or noisy? Is the listing clear about the format and track list? Are you importing a copy when a nearby shop can supply the same audio?

## Check Your Existing Copies First

Discogs shows the original 1994 *The Best Of NewOrder* master includes vinyl, CD, cassette, and video-era formats, with European 2LP pressings already in the database. If you have been collecting New Order for a while, there is a real chance you already own some version of this material.

Before ordering, check:

- Do you already own the 1994 compilation?
- Do you already own the studio albums that contain your favourite tracks?
- Do you already own original 12-inch singles or previous remix releases?

If the answer is yes, the new remaster may still be worth buying, but the reason should be precise: a clean modern play copy, the Abbey Road cut, the expanded 3LP *Rest Of*, or a replacement for an old noisy copy.

What you want to avoid is the classic reissue trap: buying again because the listing is new, then discovering that your shelf already had the version you actually needed.

This is where a collection app helps. In My Vinyls, add edition notes for the copy you own or plan to buy: black 2LP, blue store exclusive, pink 3LP, 2026 remaster, Abbey Road cut, retailer, price paid, and any condition notes after the first play. Future you will thank present you when another New Order reissue cycle appears.

## When the CD Deluxe Set Makes More Sense

Vinyl collectors do not always like hearing this, but the 4CD deluxe set may be the better archive purchase for some people.

Rough Trade says the 4CD version combines the original albums with remastered audio plus two extra CDs of rare vinyl-only tracks and unreleased remixes. Classic Pop also notes the expanded remix material. If your main interest is completeness, the CD box may give you more music and more context for less shelf stress than trying to assemble every vinyl version.

That does not make the vinyl editions redundant. The vinyl versions are the better tactile objects and the better way to sit with each side as a sequence. But if you are chasing rare mixes rather than the physical ritual, compare the CD track list before assuming vinyl is automatically the correct format.

The healthiest collecting rule is simple: buy the format that matches how you will use the music.

## The Bottom Line

If you want one New Order purchase this week, the 2LP *Best Of* is the sensible starting point. It is playable, focused, and gives you the singles in a format that belongs on a working shelf.

If you already have the hits covered and care about remix culture, the 3LP *Rest Of* is the more interesting collector move. If you want every rare mix, check the 4CD deluxe before spending vinyl money. And if the blue or pink editions are calling to you, make sure you are choosing them because they fit your collection, not because the checkout page made the decision feel urgent.

The best reissue purchase is not the loudest variant. It is the one you can explain in one sentence when you file it.
