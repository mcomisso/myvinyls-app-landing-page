Every year, the RSD list drops and the internet collectively loses its mind trying to figure out which of the 350-odd titles are worth queuing for at dawn. Most of the list is fine. Some of it is filler. And a handful of releases are genuinely excellent.

I've gone through the full RSD 2026 catalogue, cross-referenced pressing details, checked what's actually scarce versus what just looks limited, and landed on 15 titles that I think justify waking up early on April 18.

Fair warning: this list is opinionated. Yours would be different. That's the point.

## The Big Names

### 1. Taylor Swift - "Elizabeth Taylor" (7-inch single)

Let's get this out of the way first because it'll dominate the conversation. A limited 7-inch pressing of "Elizabeth Taylor" with an exclusive B-side. The Swifties will be out in force, and this will sell out everywhere within minutes of doors opening.

Is it musically essential? Probably not. Is it going to be one of the most sought-after RSD releases in years? Without question. If you're a Swift collector, this is your number one priority. If you're not, just know that the queue in front of you is largely there for this.

### 2. The Cure - Greatest Hits (Silver Vinyl)

Greatest Hits on silver vinyl is a proper collector's piece. The Cure's compilation holds up remarkably well, and this pressing reportedly uses half-speed mastering at Abbey Road. That usually means excellent sound quality, not just a gimmick colour.

Robert Smith has been on a genuine run lately, and this feels like a celebration of the catalogue rather than a lazy repackage. One of the safest bets on the list.

### 3. Neil Young & The Chrome Hearts - "As Time Explodes" (2xLP)

Neil Young doing what Neil Young does: something unexpected. This collaboration with The Chrome Hearts runs across two discs, and early word is that it leans into the noisier, more experimental side of his work. Think *Arc Weld* more than *Harvest*.

The 2xLP format and limited run make this one that could get expensive on the aftermarket fast. If you're a Neil fan, prioritise it.

### 4. Fleetwood Mac - Peter Green Era Compilation

This one matters. Peter Green's Fleetwood Mac gets overshadowed by the Buckingham-Nicks era (understandably, given the drama and the sales numbers), but the early blues material is extraordinary. "Albatross," "Oh Well," "Black Magic Woman," "Man of the World" — these tracks are foundational.

The pressing details suggest a proper remaster from the original tapes. If that's accurate, this could be the definitive way to own these recordings on vinyl.

### 5. Primal Scream - Echo Dek

*Echo Dek* is the dub reworking of *Vanishing Point*, produced by Adrian Sherwood. It's been out of print on vinyl for ages, and this reissue has been long requested. If you know, you know. If you don't, think: Primal Scream at their most hypnotic, deconstructed and rebuilt through Sherwood's bass-heavy, echo-drenched production.

Not a casual listen. A phenomenal one.

## The Deep Cuts Worth Your Time

### 6. Tanita Tikaram - The Acoustic Album (Crystal Clear Vinyl)

Here's a pick nobody's talking about, and that's exactly why it's on this list. Tanita Tikaram's voice on vinyl, on an acoustic recording, on crystal clear wax? That's going to sound gorgeous. "Twist in My Sobriety" alone is worth the price of entry.

These under-the-radar releases are often the ones that bring the most joy. Low demand means you'll actually find a copy, and it won't cost you a fortune.

### 7. Khruangbin - Live at Pitchfork Music Festival (2xLP)

Khruangbin are one of those bands that genuinely transform in a live setting. Their studio records are great, but the live experience is transcendent. If this captures even half of what they do on stage, it'll be a go-to record for lazy Sunday afternoons.

### 8. Fontaines D.C. - Early Demos (12-inch EP)

Before *Dogrel* made them one of the most important guitar bands in years, Fontaines D.C. were recording rough demos that bristled with energy. This EP collects some of that early material, and for fans who came in later, it's a fascinating look at where the band started.

Limited to 2,000 copies, so it's genuinely scarce.

### 9. Mogwai - "Music for a Forgotten Future" (Single LP)

Mogwai doing Mogwai things: expansive, cinematic, occasionally crushing. This appears to be a collection of unreleased soundtrack work, which makes sense given how much film scoring Stuart Braithwaite and company have been doing. Their instrumental work translates beautifully to vinyl.

### 10. Cleo Sol - "Heaven" Acoustic Sessions (LP)

Cleo Sol has quietly built one of the most beautiful catalogues in contemporary soul and R&B. These acoustic reworkings strip everything back to her voice and minimal instrumentation. It's intimate, it's warm, and it's exactly the kind of thing that vinyl was made for.

### 11. Roisin Murphy - "Overpowered" (Expanded Edition, 2xLP on Pink Vinyl)

*Overpowered* is a disco-pop masterpiece that somehow still feels underappreciated. This expanded edition adds remixes and B-sides to the original tracklist. Pink vinyl is a bit on the nose, but the music is undeniable. "Let Me Know" is one of the great pop songs of the 2000s. Fight me.

## The Wildcards

### 12. John Coltrane - "Evenings at the Village Vanguard" (Mono Reissue)

If this is sourced from the original mono masters, it'll be a reference-quality jazz pressing. Coltrane at the Village Vanguard is sacred ground. Even if you own another version, a proper mono reissue from this era is worth having.

### 13. Slowdive - "Pygmalion" Sessions (LP)

*Pygmalion* is the Slowdive album that divided fans on release but has aged into something widely regarded as a quiet classic. Unreleased session material from those recordings? For shoegaze collectors, this is the deep cut of the day.

### 14. Fela Kuti - "Zombie" (Orange Vinyl Reissue)

"Zombie" is one of the most important protest albums ever made, and it sounds phenomenal on vinyl. The long-form Afrobeat jams need room to breathe, and the 12-inch format gives them exactly that. Orange vinyl feels appropriately bold for Fela.

### 15. Nils Frahm - "Spaces" (Clear Vinyl Reissue)

*Spaces* captures Nils Frahm in concert, and it's a genuinely moving listen. Piano, electronics, and the ambient sound of the room. On a good system, this record makes you feel like you're sitting in the third row. The clear vinyl pressing is fitting for music this transparent.

## What I'm Skipping

A few high-profile releases that I'm personally passing on:

The picture discs. Almost all of them. They look cool on a wall. They sound mediocre on a turntable. If you're buying to play, standard black or coloured vinyl will always win.

The mega-expensive box sets that repackage material you can find elsewhere for less. RSD should be about unique releases, not just premium packaging of greatest hits you already own.

Anything where the only selling point is the vinyl colour. Transparent purple is not a reason to spend 35 quid.

## Strategy for April 18

If you want multiple titles from this list, you need to rank them ruthlessly. Assume you'll get your top three, hope for five, and be pleasantly surprised by anything beyond that.

The Swift 7-inch, The Cure, and the Neil Young will go first. The Tanita Tikaram, the Cleo Sol, and the Nils Frahm will likely still be on shelves by midday. Plan accordingly.

And remember: RSD stock that doesn't sell on the day often shows up online through participating shops in the days after. Patience is a collector's best tool.

Good luck out there. May your queue be short and your want list be long.
