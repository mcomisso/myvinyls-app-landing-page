At this point, your Record Store Day list is probably already too long.

That's normal. The full Record Store Day 2026 release list is out, the date is locked for **Saturday, April 18**, and most collectors are now in the dangerous phase where they convince themselves they can somehow get everything.

You can't. So this post is about the part that actually matters in the final few days: giving yourself a plan that makes the morning less chaotic and a lot less expensive.

## First, Accept What Kind of RSD Buyer You Are

There are basically two ways to have a good Record Store Day.

**Type 1: You want a few specific records and you'd like to get them without drama.**
In that case, your job is to rank your list hard and keep it short.

**Type 2: You love the whole event and you're happy to queue, browse, and see what happens.**
In that case, your job is to protect yourself from impulse buys that knock out your budget by 9:15 AM.

Both approaches are valid. Problems start when you pretend you're the second type but spend like the first one is life or death.

## The Three-List Method

A single wishlist is not enough for RSD. Split it into three.

### 1. Must-have

These are the titles you'd be genuinely disappointed to miss. Keep this list short, ideally **three to five releases max**.

The 2026 list is deep enough that this is harder than usual. Coverage from NME, Stereogum, and Official Charts all points to the same thing: this year's event is packed with high-attention exclusives, including titles from artists like **Carly Rae Jepsen, Ethel Cain, Weezer, Slipknot, Air, Charli XCX, CMAT, and Bring Me The Horizon**.

If you put 15 records in your must-have list, you don't have a must-have list. You have a fantasy.

### 2. Nice-to-have

These are records you'd happily buy if they're there, but missing them won't ruin your day.

This list matters because it stops you from panic-buying random titles just because your top pick sold out.

### 3. Post-RSD watchlist

Some titles will be gone immediately. Others will quietly linger, get restocked, or show up later on shop sites where allowed. A separate watchlist keeps you patient and stops you paying flipper prices before breakfast.

## Check Your Shop, Not Just the Global List

This sounds obvious, but loads of people still plan their day around the master release list instead of the actual shop they're visiting.

Record Store Day's official release list confirms what's available for the event, not what your local shop definitely ordered. Official Charts has also confirmed that **more than 300 indie shops across the UK** are taking part this year, but stock depth will vary massively from store to store.

Some shops publish full incoming stock lists. Some tease highlights on Instagram. Some stay deliberately vague. Either way, the smart move is to check your shop's channels now, not on Friday night.

If your local store looks thin on your priority titles, it's better to know early and adjust than to discover it while standing in the queue.

## Set a Real Budget Before You Leave Home

This is where people get themselves.

Limited 7-inches, double LP live sets, deluxe editions, picture discs, soundtrack packages, one "while I'm here" catalogue pickup, coffee, lunch, another record because the person in front of you looked excited about it. Suddenly the total makes you feel a bit sick.

Set your number now. Then split it.

- **70% for your must-haves**
- **20% for nice-to-haves**
- **10% for surprises**

That last 10% is important. It gives you room to enjoy the day without turning the whole thing into financial nonsense.

## Prepare for the Queue Like an Adult

Record Store Day is fun. Standing outside for ages while underdressed and under-caffeinated is not.

A few boring things that make the day better:

- Check your shop's opening time and queue policy
- Bring a tote bag that can handle a few records safely
- Charge your phone fully
- Bring water
- Wear layers
- If your shop prefers cards, great. If not, carry backup payment

This is especially worth taking seriously in the UK, where April weather loves making collectors regret their choices.

## Don't Let Secondary Market Hype Set Your Priorities

Some RSD titles are genuinely scarce. Some are just loudly discussed.

That's a big difference.

Every year, a handful of releases become social-media shorthand for "the one everyone wants," and collectors start chasing noise instead of buying records they actually care about. Coverage of the 2026 list has already centered heavily on a few headline exclusives, which is useful as a temperature check, but not a reason by itself to spend money.

If a record matters to you, go for it. If it only matters because everyone online is talking about it, give yourself ten minutes before taking it to the counter.

## The Best RSD Mornings Usually Aren't Perfect

You might miss your top title.

You might leave with something unexpected instead.

You might spend an hour talking to other collectors in the queue and realise that's half the appeal anyway. That's not failure. That's Record Store Day working as intended.

The goal isn't to "win" the morning. It's to come back with a few records you're actually excited to play, and ideally without buying a duplicate or blowing next month's record budget.

If you want one final rule, make it this: **buy for your shelf, not for the screenshot**.

That rule ages well.
